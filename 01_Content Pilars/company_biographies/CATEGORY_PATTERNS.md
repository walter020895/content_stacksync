# Category Patterns: How to Create Curated Stories About Similar Companies

This analysis identifies the common threads that connect company origin stories within each category, enabling you to create compelling curated narratives.

---

## CRM COMPANIES: The "Frustrated User" Pattern

### The Universal Origin Story
**Every CRM founder was a frustrated user of existing CRMs first.**

| Company | The Frustration | What They Hated |
|---------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Salesforce** | Marc Benioff at Oracle | Enterprise software installed from CDs, expensive, complex |
| **HubSpot** | Brian Halligan at Longworth Ventures | Cold calls, purchased email lists—outbound marketing dying |
| **Pipedrive** | Timo Rein at VAIN & Partners | Salesforce complexity for a small training business |
| **Close** | Steli Efti at Elastic Sales | "Every CRM on the market is garbage" for actual salespeople |
| **Freshworks** | Girish Mathrubootham at Zoho | $500 TV replacement disaster + Zendesk price hikes |
| **Zoho** | Sridhar Vembu | Enterprise tools priced out of reach for SMBs |
| **Attio** | Nicolas Sharp at Passion Capital | VCs evaluating software couldn't find good software for themselves |
| **Affinity** | Ray Zhou & Shubham Goel | 50 interviews revealed everyone struggles with relationship management |

### The Shared Narrative Arc

**ACT 1: The Personal Pain**
- Founder works in sales/marketing/relationships
- Uses existing tools daily
- Reaches breaking point with complexity, price, or usability

**ACT 2: The "Why Not Us?" Moment**
- Realizes they understand the problem better than incumbents
- Often builds internal tool first (Close, Pipedrive)
- Or sees market signal (Freshworks: Hacker News comment)

**ACT 3: The Anti-Incumbent Positioning**
- Salesforce = "too complex"
- Enterprise tools = "too expensive"
- Legacy CRMs = "built for managers, not users"

### Curated Story Framework for CRM

**"The CRM Revolution: How Frustrated Users Built the Tools They Wished Existed"**

1. **The Common Enemy**: Salesforce and enterprise complexity
   - Every story features founders who tried Salesforce and found it overwhelming for their actual needs

2. **The Underdog Geography**: Built outside Silicon Valley
   - Chennai (Zoho, Freshworks)
   - Estonia (Pipedrive)
   - London (Attio)
   - Remote-first (Close)
   - Proves you don't need SF to build world-class software

3. **The Pricing Revolution**: Democratizing access
   - Zoho One: 45 apps for less than one Salesforce license
   - Freshworks: Built to take Zendesk's customers at better prices
   - Pipedrive/Close: Self-serve, no enterprise sales teams needed

4. **The "Internal Tool" Origin**: Many started as internal solutions
   - Close: Built for Elastic Sales, customers asked to buy it
   - Pipedrive: Built to run their own training business
   - HubSpot: Blog first to prove inbound marketing worked

## DATABASE COMPANIES: The "Engineer Rebellion" Pattern

### The Universal Origin Story
**Database founders are engineers who saw architectural limitations that annoyed them.**

| Company | The Insight | The Rebellion Against |
|---------|-------------|----------------------|
| **MongoDB** | Documents, not rows | Oracle's rigid relational model |
| **PostgreSQL** | Extensibility as first principle | Ingres's inflexibility |
| **MySQL** | Speed over features | Oracle's expensive complexity |
| **MariaDB** | Fork to preserve freedom | Oracle's acquisition of MySQL |
| **CockroachDB** | Spanner for everyone | Google keeping Spanner private |
| **Supabase** | Open Firebase | Google locking in developers |
| **Firebase** | Real-time sync | Backend complexity for frontend devs |
| **MotherDuck** | Most data isn't "big" | BigQuery overkill for small datasets |

### The Shared Narrative Arc

**ACT 1: The Technical Observation**
- Engineer works at large tech company (Google, Oracle, IBM)
- Sees either a limitation OR an internal tool that doesn't exist outside
- Gets frustrated that the world can't access what they can

**ACT 2: The "Build It Ourselves" Moment**
- CockroachDB: "I saw Spanner at Google. I'll build it for everyone."
- Supabase: "Firebase is great but locked in. We'll open-source it."
- MotherDuck: "BigQuery founder realizes most people don't have big data"

**ACT 3: The Open Source Lever**
- Most database companies use open source as distribution
- Community builds trust before commercial pressure
- PostgreSQL, MySQL, MongoDB all started open source

### Curated Story Framework for Databases

**"The Database Rebels: How Engineers Escaped Giants to Build for Everyone"**

1. **The Google/Oracle Exit Pattern**:
   - CockroachDB (ex-Google, saw Spanner)
   - MotherDuck (ex-Google BigQuery)
   - Databricks (Berkeley AMPLab, not Google but academic)
   - MongoDB (DoubleClick/Gilt veterans)

2. **The Fork as Liberation**:
   - MariaDB: Monty forked his own creation to save it from Oracle
   - PostgreSQL: Academic project freed from Berkeley

3. **The Naming Quirks**:
   - MySQL: Named after daughter My
   - MariaDB: Named after daughter Maria
   - MongoDB: "humongous" shortened
   - CockroachDB: "Nearly impossible to kill"

4. **The "Democratization" Theme**:
   - Every database story is about making something accessible
   - Spanner → CockroachDB (Google tech for everyone)
   - Firebase → Supabase (Google service made open)

## ERP/FINANCE: The "Kitchen Table" Pattern

### The Universal Origin Story
**ERP/Finance founders often started from observing personal or family financial frustration.**

| Company | The Observation | The Context |
|---------|-----------------|-------------|
| **QuickBooks** | Wife struggling with checkbook | Scott Cook watching at kitchen table |
| **SAP** | IBM cancelled their project | Five engineers left to build it themselves |
| **Workday** | Oracle hostile takeover of PeopleSoft | Dave Duffield's revenge |
| **NetSuite** | Five-minute call with Larry Ellison | Evan Goldberg got funding and started |
| **Chargebee** | "Everyone needs to get paid" | Seven-year savings plan before starting |

### The Shared Narrative Arc

**ACT 1: The Personal/Corporate Moment**
- Kitchen table observation (QuickBooks)
- Cancelled project (SAP)
- Corporate betrayal (Workday)

**ACT 2: The Long Game**
- Chargebee: Saved 20-30% of salary for 7 years before starting
- SAP: Five engineers quit IBM together
- These are patient founders, not move-fast-break-things types

**ACT 3: The Enterprise Ambition**
- Unlike CRM, these target large businesses from the start
- Enterprise sales cycles, implementation complexity
- Often compete directly with Oracle

### Curated Story Framework for ERP/Finance

**"The Patient Builders: How Long-Term Thinking Created Finance Infrastructure"**

1. **The Delayed Gratification Pattern**:
   - Chargebee: 7 years of saving before founding
   - SAP: Decades of building from Germany
   - Contrast with VC "grow fast" culture

2. **The Revenge/Liberation Theme**:
   - Workday: Dave Duffield's revenge against Oracle
   - SAP: Building what IBM cancelled
   - NetSuite: Larry Ellison funded a competitor to Oracle (irony)

3. **The "Everyone Needs X" Insight**:
   - Chargebee: "Everyone needs to get paid"
   - QuickBooks: Everyone has a checkbook to balance
   - Simple universal truths at the foundation

## Cross-Category Patterns

### 1. The Geographic Underdog
Companies that prove you don't need Silicon Valley:
- **Chennai**: Zoho, Freshworks, Chargebee
- **Estonia**: Pipedrive
- **Germany**: SAP
- **Copenhagen**: Zendesk
- **France**: Pigment

**Curated Story**: "The Global Software Revolution: How Non-Valley Founders Built Billion-Dollar Companies"

### 2. The Bootstrapped Rebels
Companies that said NO to VC:
- **Zoho**: Rejected $140M at $140M valuation—now worth billions
- **Close**: 100% bootstrapped to $50M+ revenue
- **Mailchimp** (adjacent): Sold for $12B, never took VC

**Curated Story**: "The Anti-VC Playbook: How Patient Builders Kept Control"

### 3. The Internal Tool → Product
Companies that built for themselves first:
- **Close**: Elastic Sales internal tool
- **Pipedrive**: Their own sales training business
- **ConnectWise**: 15 years as MSP before selling the software
- **Slack** (adjacent): Internal tool at Tiny Speck

**Curated Story**: "Accidental Products: When Internal Tools Become Billion-Dollar Businesses"

### 4. The Pivot Survivors
Companies that started as something else:
- **Firebase**: Chat widget → Backend platform
- **Shopify**: Snowboard shop → E-commerce platform
- **Supabase**: Changed one word in tagline → 100x growth
- **Close**: SwipeGood failed → Elastic Sales → Close CRM

**Curated Story**: "The Pivot Masters: How Failed Ideas Became Legendary Companies"

## How to Create a Curated Category Story

### Template Structure

**1. The Opening Paradox**
Start with what seems contradictory:
- "The best CRM founders were people who hated CRMs"
- "The biggest database companies were built by people who left the biggest database companies"
- "The most patient founders in tech built financial software"

**2. The Pattern Recognition**
Show the reader you've noticed something they haven't:
- "Look at HubSpot, Pipedrive, Close, Freshworks, and Attio. Every single founder was a frustrated user first."

**3. The Timeline/Geography Map**
Create a visual narrative:
- "1996: Chennai. Zoho is born. 2010: Chennai. Freshworks is born. 2011: Chennai. Chargebee founders start saving."

**4. The Counter-Narrative**
Challenge conventional startup wisdom:
- "YC says ship fast. Attio went silent for three years. Zoho rejected $140M. Chargebee saved for seven years."

**5. The Synthesis Insight**
What does this category teach us?
- CRM: "Build for users, not buyers"
- Databases: "Democratize what giants hoard"
- Finance: "Patience is a competitive advantage"

## Key Insight for Curated Stories

**The best curated stories reveal a hidden pattern that makes the reader say: "I never noticed that before, but now I can't unsee it."**

For CRM: The pattern is frustrated users becoming founders.
For Databases: The pattern is engineers liberating hoarded technology.
For Finance: The pattern is patient, long-term builders.

Find the pattern. Name it. Show the examples. Let the reader feel smarter for seeing it.
