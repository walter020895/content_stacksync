# AMY HOOD — BIOGRAPHICAL DOSSIER
**Subject:** Amy Elizabeth Hood (born August 9, 1971)
**Title:** Executive Vice President & Chief Financial Officer, Microsoft
**Tenure:** May 2013 — present (13 years). Longest-serving CFO in Microsoft history.
**Distinction:** First female CFO in Microsoft's history.
**Research date:** February 25, 2026

---

## 1. PERSONAL INFLECTION POINT

### Origin: Morehead, Kentucky / Nashville, Tennessee

Amy Hood grew up in Morehead, Kentucky for the first 12 years of her life, then moved to Nashville, Tennessee. Her father was a medical doctor. Her mother taught nursing. Her sister became a pediatrician. Most of her relatives were teachers.

She was raised in the community of the church — it was the center of the family's value system. She adopted the belief early that one did things for a bigger purpose, not always for oneself. Education was treated as non-negotiable. She was on the math team in school.

**Duke University (1994):** Bachelor's degree in economics.
**Harvard Business School (1999):** MBA.

### Goldman Sachs (1994-2002): Eight Years on Wall Street

Hood started at Goldman Sachs in 1994 as an analyst in investment banking, immediately after Duke. She held that position until 1997, when she left for Harvard Business School. After her MBA (1999), she rejoined Goldman as an associate in investment banking and capital markets. She stayed until 2002 — eight years total at Goldman.

### The Move to Microsoft (December 2002)

Hood joined Microsoft in December 2002 as a senior director in investor relations within corporate finance. This was not a glamorous entry point. She was leveraging her Goldman banking background to handle how Microsoft communicated its financial story to Wall Street.

From there, her career path was unusual for a future CFO:
- **Chief of Staff, Server and Tools Business** — operational, not financial
- **Strategy and Business Development lead, Business Division** — the division that owned Office, including what would become Office 365
- **CFO, Business Division (January 2010)** — she ran finance for the division that would later define Microsoft's subscription model

This is critical: Hood was embedded in the business that would become Microsoft 365 *before* she became company CFO. She understood the subscription shift from the inside.

### Becoming CFO: May 8, 2013

Microsoft announced Hood as Peter Klein's replacement on May 8, 2013. She was a Corporate Vice President at the time, not on anyone's "future CEO" list.

**What happened in her first months was chaos:**

- Weeks into the job, she flew to Europe to negotiate the purchase of Nokia's phone assets — a $9.5 billion deal she inherited, not one she championed.
- Shortly after, Steve Ballmer shook up the C-Suite, giving Hood more power over the finances of each product division.
- A month later, Ballmer announced his departure as CEO.
- It took nearly six months to replace Ballmer. During this period, Hood was the financial steward of a $300B company with no permanent CEO.

At her one-year anniversary as CFO, **the legal department gave her a plaque congratulating her for making it through the first 12 months.** That is not a normal gift. That tells you what that year was like.

### The Ballmer-to-Nadella Transition

Hood already knew Satya Nadella. He had overseen small-business accounting and customer software while she ran strategy for the larger Office group that included his business. They were adjacent, not strangers.

When Nadella took over as CEO in February 2014, Hood had already been CFO for nine months. She was the continuity. He was the change. Together, they became what observers call "a package deal" — "Satya has done an excellent job but people think of them as a package together."

## 2. THE UNSEXY WORK

### 2A. Cleaning Up Nokia ($7.6 Billion Write-Down)

The Nokia acquisition was Ballmer's last major bet. Hood inherited it weeks into her CFO role and had to fly to Europe to close it. Less than a year after the deal closed, it was foundering — missing Hood's own forecasts for sales and savings.

Her response, according to Bloomberg: "Once the forecast failed to pan out, Amy was extremely rigorous on first calling attention to the failing performance and then encouraging everyone to focus on making hard decisions quickly."

In July 2015, Nadella announced Microsoft was writing off $7.6 billion on Nokia and cutting 7,800 jobs. Hood took the write-down. She didn't hide from it. She has shown herself willing to take money away from legacy divisions such as Windows and give it to more promising businesses like the cloud group.

### 2B. The Cloud Pivot: Office 365 to Microsoft 365

This is the central financial story of Amy Hood's career, and it's the one nobody tells in human terms.

**The problem:** Microsoft's core business — Windows and Office — ran on perpetual licenses. You buy a box, Microsoft gets paid once. Revenue is lumpy, unpredictable, and declining as the market shifts.

**The bet:** Move customers to subscription-based cloud services (Office 365, Azure). Revenue becomes recurring, predictable, and grows with usage. But in the short term, you are voluntarily *shrinking* your own revenue. A $300 license becomes $10/month — it takes 2.5 years just to break even on the same customer.

**Hood's quote on the shift:** "The subscription model allowed us to create a continuous value exchange with our customers rather than episodic transactions."

**What Hood actually did:**

1. **Shifted capital allocation** — took money from Windows and legacy divisions, redirected it to cloud infrastructure and Azure. This meant telling Windows leaders their budgets were shrinking. In Microsoft's culture circa 2013, that was a political minefield.

2. **Restructured the entire sales organization** — Microsoft created a legion of highly technical salespeople embedded at customer locations to get cloud projects running and showcase Azure capabilities. Sales compensation was restructured around customer success because "ultimately in a consumption-based business, customer success is all that matters."

3. **Rebuilt Wall Street communication** — After Ballmer left, Microsoft shares had given up a third of their value during his tenure, even though sales almost quadrupled. At an executive retreat in 2015, Hood lectured senior leaders on the need for more internal and external metrics. She and Nadella then hatched a plan to refocus investors on the smaller but growing cloud business, pledging to reach $20 billion in annualized commercial cloud revenue by fiscal 2018. They passed that goal in the first quarter.

4. **Used machine learning to make accurate forecasts** — Hood turned to Microsoft's own machine-learning software, plugging hundreds of thousands of data points into algorithms to ensure her predictions to Wall Street would hold. She understood that to sell investors on new metrics, she needed to be right every time.

**Key milestone:** In Q4 of fiscal year 2017, Office 365 revenue exceeded conventional license sales of Microsoft Office for the first time. The subscription model had officially won.

**Timeline:**
- 2011: Office 365 cloud subscription launches
- Feb 2013: Office 365 expands to consumer and SMB plans
- May 2013: Amy Hood becomes CFO
- Feb 2014: Satya Nadella becomes CEO
- 2015: Hood and Nadella set $20B cloud revenue target
- Q4 FY2017: Office 365 subscription revenue surpasses perpetual license revenue
- 2019: Microsoft hits $1 trillion market cap
- April 2020: Office 365 rebranded to Microsoft 365
- Oct 2022: "Microsoft Office" brand discontinued; fully Microsoft 365
- 2024: Microsoft 365 generates over $65 billion in annual revenue, 80.8M consumer subscribers

### 2C. The Acquisitions She Greenlit

Hood has overseen more than 57 deals as CFO. Her acquisition philosophy, in her own words: "For the past five years, we've been incredibly consistent — buy communities, look for networked assets, look for growing markets, and look for where we're a better owner."

| Deal | Year | Amount | What It Bought |
|---|---|---|---|
| Nokia (phones) | 2013 | $9.5B | Write-down. Hood cleaned up Ballmer's mess. |
| LinkedIn | 2016 | $26.2B | Professional network. Largest deal at the time. |
| GitHub | 2018 | $7.5B | Developer community. "Those types of assets are always interesting." |
| Activision Blizzard | 2023 | $68.7B | Largest tech acquisition in history. Gaming + Candy Crush + community. |
| OpenAI (investment) | 2019-2023 | $13B total | 49% profit-share. AI business on pace to exceed $10B annual run rate — "the fastest business in our history to reach this milestone." |

On LinkedIn, GitHub, Minecraft (earlier acquisition): "LinkedIn connects professionals, Minecraft connects players and GitHub connects developers. Those types of assets are always interesting and always will be around the world."

### 2D. The "Don't Build a Gold Toilet" Philosophy

In a 2018 meeting, Hood told employees: **"Don't build a gold toilet."** The reference was to the tendency of wealthy tech companies to develop products for the sake of it, rather than to meet a customer's need. Vice President Catherine Gluckstein later wrote in a February 2019 email to Phil Spencer (gaming CEO): "Amy's words from the meeting in the Fall still ring in my ears — 'don't build a gold toilet.'" This came to light during the Activision antitrust trial in 2023.

### 2E. The Market Cap Journey

- **May 2013** (Hood becomes CFO): ~$300 billion market cap
- **February 2026**: ~$3 trillion market cap
- **Approximate 10x growth** under her financial stewardship
- Microsoft's stock surged almost 300% in just the first five years of her tenure

## 3. PHILANTHROPY / VALUES

### 3A. Climate: $1 Billion Innovation Fund + Internal Carbon Tax

In January 2020, Hood was one of the three key executives (alongside Nadella and Brad Smith) who announced Microsoft's commitment to be carbon negative by 2030, and to remove all carbon the company has emitted since its founding in 1975 by 2050.

**Hood's specific financial mechanisms:**
- Announced a $1 billion Climate Innovation Fund for carbon reduction, capture, and removal technologies
- Expanded Microsoft's internal carbon tax to include Scope 3 emissions (supply chain, products, customer usage). The existing fee was $15/metric ton for Scope 1 and 2. Hood added Scope 3 at $5/ton, increasing each year until parity. This is not a "shadow fee" — each division actually pays from its budget, and the funds go to sustainability improvements.

### 3B. Affordable Housing: $750 Million

In 2019, Hood and Brad Smith committed $500 million (later growing to $750 million) for affordable housing in the Puget Sound region. The investment has created or preserved 16,000 affordable housing units.

### 3C. Racial Equity Initiative

Hood leads Microsoft's effort to use its balance sheet for systemic change: bringing more Black-owned suppliers and partners into Microsoft's network, banking with more Black-owned financial institutions that in turn lend to Black-owned businesses.

### 3D. Board Service

Since 2017, Hood has served on the board of directors of 3M, sitting on its compensation and science, technology, and sustainability committees.

### 3E. Women in Tech / Finance

Hood's consistent message to women: **"Be confident, be brave, and have fun."**

At Fortune's Most Powerful Women Summit (2018): "You can rewrite what the job is about. You can bring what you have to it and then surround yourself with really awesome people who bring things that you don't have."

She advocates for inclusive leadership and mentoring, speaking regularly to women and students on finance and STEM careers.

## 5. CONNECTOR BRIDGE: Amy Hood -> Microsoft 365 -> Stacksync

### The Direct Line

Stacksync's Microsoft 365 connector enables real-time two-way sync between Microsoft 365 and 200+ other business applications (Salesforce, HubSpot, NetSuite, etc.). This connector exists because Microsoft 365 exists as a cloud SaaS platform with APIs. Microsoft 365 exists as a cloud SaaS platform because Amy Hood architected the financial transformation that moved Microsoft from perpetual licenses to cloud subscriptions.

### The Transformation Timeline (Hood's Fingerprints)

1. **Before Hood (pre-2013):** Microsoft Office was a box you bought. No API. No subscription. No connector ecosystem. Integration meant manual exports and imports.

2. **Hood becomes CFO (May 2013):** She had already been CFO of the Business Division that ran Office 365 since January 2010. She understood the subscription model's potential from the inside.

3. **Capital reallocation (2013-2015):** Hood shifted billions from Windows/legacy to cloud infrastructure. This funded the Azure platform that Microsoft 365 runs on, and the APIs that third-party connectors like Stacksync rely on.

4. **Sales restructuring (2015-2018):** Hood created embedded technical sales teams focused on cloud adoption. This drove enterprise migration from on-prem Office to Office 365, growing the customer base that third-party integrators serve.

5. **Revenue crossover (Q4 FY2017):** Office 365 subscriptions surpassed perpetual licenses. The SaaS model won. The API economy around Microsoft 365 began to explode.

6. **Microsoft 365 rebrand (April 2020):** No longer just "Office." A full productivity platform with Teams, SharePoint, OneDrive, Exchange — all cloud-native, all API-accessible, all connectable.

7. **Today:** Microsoft 365 generates $65B+ annually, 80.8M consumer subscribers, and is one of the most-integrated SaaS platforms in the world. Stacksync connects to it in real time.

**The narrative bridge:** The woman who made the numbers work for the subscription bet is the reason Microsoft 365 is a connectable platform today. Without Hood's financial architecture — the capital reallocation, the sales restructuring, the Wall Street narrative shift — Office might still be a boxed product. No cloud. No APIs. No connectors. No Stacksync integration.

## 7. POST STRATEGY

### Three Potential Hook Angles

**HOOK A: The Imposter Syndrome Arc (Emotional)**
"Microsoft's CFO says she was 'deeply uncomfortable and unqualified' for every job she ever took. She's been running the finances of the world's most valuable company for 13 years."
*Angle:* The gap between how we feel and what we're capable of. Amy Hood's career is proof that grit beats readiness. Brittany lens: the operations person who felt underqualified but kept showing up.

**HOOK B: The $7.6 Billion Cleanup (Unsexy Work)**
"Three weeks into her first CFO job, she flew to Europe to close a $9.5 billion deal she didn't want. Two years later, she wrote off $7.6 billion of it and cut 7,800 jobs. Then she turned Microsoft into a $3 trillion company."
*Angle:* The unsexy work of cleaning up someone else's mess before you can build your own thing. Brittany lens: this is what operations people do. They inherit disasters and fix them quietly.

**HOOK C: The Subscription Bet (Connector Bridge)**
"In 2013, Amy Hood bet Microsoft's future on a model that would shrink revenue in the short term. Every $300 license became $10/month. It took 4 years for subscriptions to overtake perpetual licenses. Today Microsoft 365 generates $65 billion a year."
*Angle:* The CFO who had the patience to let a better model compound. Brittany lens: the financial discipline to choose long-term recurring revenue over short-term windfalls. Connector bridge: this decision is why Microsoft 365 is a SaaS platform that tools like Stacksync can integrate with today.

### The Single Most Emotional Fact
At her one-year anniversary as CFO, the legal department gave her a plaque congratulating her for making it through the first 12 months. That's not a celebration. That's a survival acknowledgment.

### The Single Most Surprising Fact
Hood's acquisition philosophy — "buy communities, look for networked assets, look for where we're a better owner" — led to $100B+ in acquisitions (LinkedIn, GitHub, Activision, OpenAI). She sees assets as communities of people, not revenue streams. And when the urge to micromanage her own teams gets too strong, she stress-bakes structurally complex pastry cakes for her daughters.

### The "I Didn't Know That" Detail
Amy Hood and Satya Nadella both co-own the Seattle Sounders FC. They bought in together in 2019. The CEO and the CFO who transformed Microsoft into a $3 trillion company spend their weekends watching soccer together. Their professional partnership extends beyond the boardroom — they are co-investors, co-strategists, and apparently co-fans.
