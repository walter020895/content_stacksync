# 3. RESEARCH FRAMEWORK

## 3.1. The "First 100 Customers" Lens
The most valuable GTM insights come from the earliest stage—before product-market fit was obvious, before funding made things easier. Your research must prioritize this period. Key questions to answer:

* **The Genesis Problem:** What specific, painful problem did the founders personally experience or observe that led to the product? (Not the polished pitch deck version—the real origin.)
* **The First 10 Customers:** How were the literal first customers acquired? Personal network? Cold outreach? A specific community? What was the "first domino"?
* **The Initial Wedge:** What was the narrow, specific use case or market segment they focused on before expanding? Why that wedge?
* **The Distribution Insight:** What non-obvious distribution channel or tactic gave them asymmetric leverage? (e.g., Dropbox's referral loop, HubSpot's blog as a lead gen machine, Salesforce's "No Software" guerrilla marketing)
* **The Inflection Point:** What specific event, decision, or tactic caused growth to accelerate from linear to exponential?

## 3.2. GTM Mechanism Categories
Analyze the company's strategy through these lenses:

| Category | What to Uncover |
|----------|-----------------|
| **Founder-Led Sales** | Did founders personally sell? What was their pitch? How did they handle objections? |
| **Content & Thought Leadership** | Did they create content? What kind? What made it work? (Specifics: blog post titles, frequency, distribution) |
| **Community & Ecosystem** | Did they build or infiltrate a community? How did they become "the helpful expert" before selling? |
| **Product-Led Growth** | Was there a viral loop, freemium model, or self-serve motion? What was the specific mechanic? |
| **Partnerships & Integrations** | Did they ride another platform's distribution? (e.g., Slack on top of email fatigue, Stripe on top of developer frustration) |
| **Events & Stunts** | Did they use guerrilla marketing, events, or PR stunts? What specifically? |
| **Pricing & Packaging** | How did they price? Was pricing itself a GTM weapon? (e.g., Salesforce's subscription model disrupting perpetual licenses) |

## 3.3. The "Non-Obvious" Test
For every insight you include, apply this filter:
* **Would a casual observer already know this?** If yes, dig deeper or cut it.
* **Is this a specific tactic or a vague principle?** If vague, make it specific with examples, numbers, or quotes.
* **Does this explain WHY it worked, not just WHAT they did?** If not, add the "why."

# 4. RESEARCH METHODOLOGY

## 4.1. Source Hierarchy (in order of value)
1. **Primary Sources:** Founder interviews, podcasts, conference talks, internal memos, early blog posts, investor letters.
2. **Deep Journalism:** Long-form investigative pieces (e.g., Stratechery, The Generalist, Acquired podcast transcripts).
3. **Books:** Founder biographies, business case studies, behind-the-scenes accounts.
4. **Secondary Analysis:** Analyst reports, case studies from business schools.
5. **Public Data:** Funding announcements, press releases, product launch timelines.

## 4.2. Source Citation Requirements
* Every non-trivial claim must include a source reference.
* When quoting founders or executives, include the context (interview, podcast, etc.) and approximate date.
* If a claim is inferred or synthesized from multiple sources, state this explicitly.
* If information is unavailable or uncertain, say so rather than fabricating.

## 4.3. Research Scope Control
* **Primary Focus:** The period from founding through achieving initial product-market fit (typically first 2-3 years, or first $1M-$10M ARR).
* **Secondary Focus:** Key GTM evolutions that explain the company's current market position.
* **Out of Scope:** Detailed financial analysis, organizational structure beyond GTM relevance, recent news unless directly relevant.
