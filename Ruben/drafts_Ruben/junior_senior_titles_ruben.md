### FULL POST (using Hook A)

---

A "Senior" engineer with 15 years of experience.

Sounds like a safe hire.

Until you realize 10 of those years were spent mastering tools that AI replaced in six months.

We have a language problem in tech. Junior and Senior are not skill labels. They're age labels wrapped in corporate packaging.

Look at what "Senior" actually signals in most hiring processes. Years in the industry. Time at previous companies. Familiarity with legacy systems.

Now look at what matters in 2026. Speed of learning. Willingness to rebuild workflows from scratch. Comfort with tools that didn't exist 12 months ago.

The Senior engineer knows the old system inside out. The Junior engineer never learned the old system. They started with the new one.

One carries 15 years of institutional knowledge. The other carries zero assumptions.

In a market that reinvents itself every 18 months, zero assumptions is the advantage.

I'm not saying experience is worthless. Pattern recognition matters. But patterns from a different era become anchors, not accelerators.

I've seen engineers with 20 years of experience struggle with tools that a recent grad picks up in an afternoon. The talent is the same. The baggage isn't.

That's only half the shift.

A moat used to take a decade to build. Learn the stack, accumulate tribal knowledge, become irreplaceable through institutional memory.

Today, someone with 18 months of focused AI-native work can build the same competitive depth.

Junior and Senior were labels designed for a world where knowledge compounded slowly.

That world is gone.

If you're hiring, stop filtering by years of experience. Start filtering by learning velocity.

The best engineers I've met this year had the least impressive resumes and the most impressive output.
