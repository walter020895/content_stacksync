## IDEA LEGOS

### Lego 0 — Insight (World Knowledge)
Two French engineers spent 16 years at Oracle, left because they felt like dinosaurs, bet against the entire Hadoop consensus when every investor said it was pointless, and built the largest software IPO in history from a tiny office across from a hamburger joint with IKEA furniture their kids helped assemble.

### Lego 1 — Topic
Snowflake's contrarian founding. The human story of betting against consensus when everyone tells you you're wrong.

Founder conviction. Products aren't planned in boardrooms. They're drawn on whiteboards by engineers who refuse to die comfortable. "We created a company as collateral damage" is the purest founder statement ever made. European founders who didn't need Silicon Valley permission or Larry Ellison's blessing.

## FULL POST (as published)

Snowflake started with a confession between two friends.

"I'm going to die at Oracle. And I'm probably going to die as a dinosaur."

Benoit Dageville said that to Thierry Cruanes. They'd spent over a decade at Oracle. Same architecture. Same problems. Same fixes. They were good at it and that was the trap.

So they left. Together.

Snowflake's own YouTube channel calls it a bromance. Dageville just calls Cruanes "the most optimistic guy I know."

They recruited Marcin Żukowski from the Netherlands, rented a tiny office in San Mateo across from a hamburger joint, and started drawing on a whiteboard. Their kids helped assemble the IKEA furniture.

For two years, the whiteboard was the entire company. No product. No customers. No revenue.

Their bet was simple and everyone hated it: build a cloud-native data warehouse instead of Hadoop. In 2012, that was heresy. Hadoop was consensus. Cloudera was raising massive rounds. Every pitch meeting ended the same way "Hadoop will eat you. There's no point."

But they kept building.

Cloud won. Hadoop didn't.

Sutter Hill Ventures had bet $5 million on them when nobody else would. On IPO day, that $5 million was worth $12.6 billion.

Dageville: "For us, it was about building a product, not a company. We created a company as collateral damage."

Even Berkshire Hathaway — that hadn't bought into an IPO in over 60 years. Did for Snowflake.

Two French engineers. A whiteboard across from a hamburger joint. $70 billion on day one.
